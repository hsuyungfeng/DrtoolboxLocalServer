Diabetes Mellitus: Comprehensive Clinical Guide

Chapter 1: Definition and Classification

Diabetes mellitus is a metabolic disorder characterized by elevated blood glucose levels, either due to insufficient insulin production (Type 1) or insulin resistance (Type 2).

Type 1 Diabetes
- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Typically presents in childhood or young adulthood
- Requires insulin therapy for survival
- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases

Type 2 Diabetes
- Insulin resistance with progressive beta cell dysfunction
- Usually develops in adults over 45 years
- Can often be managed with lifestyle modifications and oral medications
- Accounts for 90-95% of all diabetes cases

Gestational Diabetes
- Develops during pregnancy, typically in second or third trimester
- Increases risk of Type 2 diabetes later in life
- Requires careful monitoring during pregnancy

Chapter 2: Clinical Presentation and Diagnosis

Common Symptoms
- Increased thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Unexplained weight loss
- Fatigue and weakness
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing infections

Diagnostic Criteria
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L)
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) during oral glucose tolerance test
- HbA1c ≥6.5% (48 mmol/mol)
- Random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL in symptomatic patients

Chapter 3: Treatment Protocols

Lifestyle Management (First-line for Type 2)
- Dietary modification: reduce refined carbohydrates, increase fiber
- Regular exercise: 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity activity
- Weight loss: 5-10% reduction improves insulin sensitivity
- Stress management and adequate sleep

Pharmacological Treatment

Type 1 Diabetes
- Insulin therapy required: multiple daily injections or insulin pump
- Rapid-acting, long-acting, and basal insulin regimens
- Target HbA1c: <7% for most patients

Type 2 Diabetes (Stepwise Approach)
1. Metformin: first-line agent, improves insulin sensitivity
2. Add second agent if needed: sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors
3. Triple therapy or insulin if glycemic targets not met

Chapter 4: Complications and Prevention

Acute Complications
- Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA): life-threatening, requires immediate hospitalization
- Hyperosmolar hyperglycemic state (HHS): severe hyperglycemia with dehydration
- Severe hypoglycemia: blood glucose <70 mg/dL with neurological symptoms

Chronic Complications
- Retinopathy: leading cause of blindness in working-age adults
- Nephropathy: progressive kidney damage, may require dialysis
- Neuropathy: peripheral nerve damage causing pain and loss of sensation
- Cardiovascular disease: increased risk of MI and stroke
- Foot ulcers: amputation risk if not managed

Prevention Strategies
- Tight glycemic control (HbA1c target 6.5-7%)
- Blood pressure management (<130/80 mmHg)
- Lipid management: statin therapy for most patients
- Regular screening: annual eye exams, annual urine microalbumin, foot inspections
- Smoking cessation

Chapter 5: Patient Education

Key Learning Points
- Understanding carbohydrate counting and meal planning
- Self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG) technique and frequency
- Medication adherence and proper injection technique
- Recognition and treatment of hypoglycemia
- Sick day management
- Importance of regular follow-up and screening

Monitoring and Follow-up
- HbA1c assessment every 3 months
- Quarterly visits initially, then 2-4 times yearly once stable
- Annual comprehensive eye exam
- Annual urine microalbumin and serum creatinine
- Annual foot examination
